Marine Spatial Planning - West Bengal

Geospatial backbone of India's National Coastal and Marine Spatial Planning (CMSP) Framework — Blue Economy sector mapping and conflict-compatible zone analysis for India's coastline

NCSCM · MoEF&CC · NITI Aayog · World Bank
Marine Spatial Planning Blue Economy Conflict-Compatibility Analysis Coastal Zonation
Project

The Ministry of Environment, Forest and Climate Change (MoEF&CC) has been designated as the nodal ministry to prepare the National Coastal and Marine Spatial Planning (CMSP) Framework — a practical, science-based tool to manage India's coastal and marine space, balancing development demands with environmental protection through transparent planning. This initiative follows the constitution of a Blue Economy Coordination Committee (BECC) by NITI Aayog in April 2021, which harmonises initiatives across six functional clusters of the Blue Economy. India's coastline of 11,098 km and EEZ of 2.01 million km² host diverse ecosystems — coral reefs, mangroves, mudflats, lagoons, and estuaries — alongside 200 ports, major fisheries, offshore energy, and densely populated urban centres, all competing for the same finite marine space. At NCSCM, I contributed to building the geospatial backbone of this Framework — compiling multi-source satellite and field datasets, producing thematic maps, and delivering the conflict-compatibility and marine management zone analysis for the West Bengal coastline as India's first replicable CMSP pilot.

Objective

The National Goal of the CMSP is to promote sustainable, effective, and productive uses of India's coastal and maritime areas to contribute to the national economy and well-being by:

  • Ensuring the conservation and wise use of coastal and marine ecosystems to provide sustained delivery of ecosystem services
  • Reducing user conflicts by involving coastal communities and minimising environmental impacts through effective regulatory and management processes
  • Enhancing Blue Economy (BE) investments through systematic mapping, planning, and spatial zoning
  • Strengthening local, regional, national, intergovernmental, and international collaborations for integrated coastal governance

Data Used

Preparation of the CMSP required diverse datasets across five categories for comprehensive baseline mapping:

  • Ecological, Environmental & Oceanographic Parameters — Mangrove, seagrass, coral reef, and mudflat locations; biodiversity hotspots; Marine Protected Areas; biological production zones; oceanographic variables (sea surface temperature, salinity, currents, bathymetry)
  • Human Activities Data — Port and harbour operations; shipping lanes; fisheries catch zones; aquaculture sites; tourism corridors; industrial and urban footprints along the coast (Landsat, Sentinel-2, IRS LISS III/IV)
  • Risk & Mitigation Data — Cyclone track histories; storm surge inundation zones; coastal erosion rates; tsunami hazard maps; climate change sea-level rise projections
  • Spatial & Administrative Data — UNCLOS maritime boundaries; Territorial Waters (12 NM) and EEZ (200 NM) limits; CRZ Notification 2019 zones; administrative boundaries; existing Coastal Zone Management Plan (CZMP) data for Gujarat, Odisha, and West Bengal
  • Socio-economic Data — Census of India 2011 coastal population data; livelihoods of fishing communities; infrastructure mapping; economic indicators for Blue Economy sectors

GIS Platforms: ArcGIS Pro  ·  QGIS  ·  Google Earth Engine  ·  ArcPy automation

Process — Five-Phase CMSP Framework

The IOC-UNESCO Step-by-Step MSP framework was adapted into five major phases for India's national and local needs:

  • Phase I — Visioning
    Defined national CMSP goals; established the authority and financial resources; identified nine key Blue Economy sectors from the Low Tide Line to the EEZ (200 NM)
  • Phase II — Defining & Analysing Baselines
    Compiled multi-source spatial datasets (Landsat, Sentinel-2, IRS, Census) into a comprehensive GIS geodatabase; produced thematic layers — LULC, Geomorphology, DEM, Drainage, Soil, Mangroves, MPAs, and socio-economic layers
  • Phase III — Future Planning Process
    Identified stakeholders across all BE sectors; conducted conflict-compatibility analysis using the inter-sectoral matrix (Table 4.7); assessed thresholds for resource exploitation and carrying capacity indicators
  • Phase IV — Coastal & Marine Spatial Planning
    Defined management zones from the Low Tide Line to the EEZ, categorised as: Ecologically Important Areas · Eco-environmentally Sensitive Areas · Sustainable Economic Growth Areas · Buffer Areas; produced the West Bengal Marine Management Zone pilot map (Figure 5.4)
  • Phase V — Monitoring, Evaluation & Learning (MEL)
    Designed a GIS-driven MEL framework with progress indicators, base year mapping (2020), and implementation milestones up to the 2030 target year for full CMSP rollout

Key Results

The project produced a structured framework and action plan for CMSP across India, with the following key outputs:

  • National CMSP Framework & Action Plan (2022) — Published by MoEF&CC, Government of India, July 2022; provides the national roadmap for preparing and implementing CMSP for any coastal stretch in India
  • Five-Phase Implementation Guide — Adapted IOC-UNESCO approach into a structured Indian context; each phase has defined tasks, activities, deliverables, and stakeholder engagement requirements
  • Sector-wise Blue Economy Component Maps — Detailed existing and emerging BE sectors (fisheries, aquaculture, tourism, shipping, energy, mining, salt, coastal infrastructure, ecosystem services) from the Low Tide Line to the 200 NM EEZ with exploitation thresholds and carrying capacity indicators
  • Conflict-Compatibility Matrix (Table 4.7) — A 17×17 inter-sectoral matrix identifying compatible, partially compatible, conflicting, and independent activities across the West Bengal coast — providing planners with an evidence base for spatial separation decisions
  • Marine Management Zone Map — West Bengal Pilot (Figure 5.4) — India's first demarcated Marine Management Zones along the West Bengal coast, categorised into Conservation Zones, Preservation Zones, and Regulated Development Zones (fishing, mariculture, eco-tourism, blue carbon, navigation, energy, waste management, island development, dredging)
  • Governance & Legal Framework — Institutional arrangements, international cooperation recommendations, and legislative mapping integrating CRZ 2019, UNCLOS, the Forest Act, Wildlife Protection Act, and the new Draft National Geospatial Policy (2021)
