Satellite-Derived Bathymetry for Remote Island Ecosystems of INDIA

Accurate nearshore bathymetric mapping is essential for the management and monitoring of remote island ecosystems, yet traditional hydrographic surveying in these regions is often restricted by high costs and significant logistical challenges. To address this, a novel methodology was developed through a collaborative effort between the Space Applications Centre (SAC), Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO), to derive bathymetry for remote Indian island chains, including the Lakshadweep archipelago and the Gulf of Mannar.

SAC, ISRO · NCCR · MoES
Satellite Bathymetry ICESat-2 · ATL03 Sentinel-2 MSI
About this Project

Remote island coastlines — Lakshadweep, Gulf of Mannar — are among the most ecologically important and scientifically under-mapped coastal environments in India. Traditional ship-borne hydrographic surveys require substantial logistical coordination and financial investment to reach these areas, resulting in large data gaps in nearshore bathymetry that are critical for coastal management, reef conservation, and navigation safety. This study presents a scalable, cloud-based Satellite-Derived Bathymetry (SDB) framework that fuses NASA's ICESat-2 photon-counting LiDAR with Sentinel-2 multispectral imagery in Google Earth Engine — delivering accurate nearshore depth maps for remote tropical clear-water coastal environments at a fraction of the cost and time of conventional surveys. The methodology was developed and validated in collaboration with the Space Applications Centre (SAC), ISRO.

Objective

Develop a scalable, cost-effective framework for deriving nearshore bathymetry for remote Indian island ecosystems — specifically the Lakshadweep archipelago and the Gulf of Mannar:

  • Bypass conventional survey limitations — Overcome the substantial logistical and financial burdens inherent in traditional ship-borne hydrographic surveys by leveraging high-revisit satellite imagery and open-source cloud computing in Google Earth Engine
  • Fuse active LiDAR with passive multispectral data — Integrate NASA ICESat-2 photon-counting LiDAR (532 nm green laser, ~40 m depth penetration) with Sentinel-2 10 m multispectral imagery to derive calibrated depth measurements across the shallow nearshore zone
  • Validate as a viable monitoring solution — Demonstrate that satellite-derived bathymetry provides statistically reliable depth estimates for dynamic, clear-water coastal environments and establish it as a replicable model for remote Indian island chains

Tools & Technical Framework

The framework integrates three core data and computational components:

  • Cloud Computing — Google Earth Engine (GEE)
    Scalable geospatial analysis platform enabling cloud-based processing of large satellite archives without local computational infrastructure; used for image compositing, spectral band extraction, and model application across the study areas
  • NASA ICESat-2 (ATL03 product)
    Photon-counting LiDAR operating at 532 nm green laser wavelength with depth penetration capability up to ~40 m in clear water; ATL03 global geolocated photon data used as high-accuracy bathymetric ground-truth control points for model calibration
  • Sentinel-2 MSI (10 m resolution)
    European Space Agency multispectral imagery providing high-resolution spatial coverage; Band 2 (Blue) and Band 3 (Green) used in the Log-Ratio Model for spectral attenuation-to-depth conversion across Lakshadweep and Gulf of Mannar
  • Log-Ratio Model — Stumpf et al. (2003)
    Implemented via Python within GEE; translates the ratio of spectral radiance in the blue and green bands into water depth estimates calibrated against ICESat-2 laser returns — a physically-based, analytically robust approach for optically shallow tropical waters

Methodological Process

The workflow integrates multispectral passive remote sensing with active spaceborne LiDAR across four structured steps:

  • Step I — Data Fusion in Google Earth Engine
    Harmonised ICESat-2 ATL03 photon-counting LiDAR data with Sentinel-2 multispectral imagery within the GEE environment. Sentinel-2 imagery was filtered for cloud cover (<5%), atmospherically corrected, and co-registered with ICESat-2 ground tracks over Lakshadweep and Gulf of Mannar study areas
  • Step II — Log-Ratio Model Implementation
    Applied the Stumpf et al. (2003) Log-Ratio Model using Sentinel-2 Band 2 (Blue) and Band 3 (Green) reflectance values. The model equation — Z = m₁ × ln(Rrs_blue) / ln(Rrs_green) − m₀ — was implemented in Python via the GEE API, generating a continuous depth raster across the shallow nearshore zone
  • Step III — ICESat-2 Calibration
    Leveraged ICESat-2 high-precision green laser returns as ground-truth control points to calibrate the Log-Ratio Model coefficients (m₁, m₀). Extracted photon elevations from ATL03 product along satellite ground tracks, filtered for seafloor returns, and applied tidal correction to convert to chart datum
  • Step IV — Validation & Accuracy Assessment
    Performed rigorous cross-referencing of model-derived depths against independent field-survey measurements and nautical chart data. Computed R², RMSE, and bias statistics to quantify model performance and assess spatial distribution of depth prediction errors across the Gulf of Mannar calibration site
Output Results

Figure 1 — Satellite-Derived Bathymetry Output · Gulf of Mannar
Nearshore depth map derived from the Log-Ratio Model (Stumpf et al., 2003) applied to Sentinel-2 Band 2/3 imagery, calibrated against ICESat-2 ATL03 photon-counting LiDAR returns. Depth contours from 0–40 m mapped at 10 m spatial resolution. Calibration achieved R² = 0.691 against independent field measurements — validating satellite-derived bathymetry as a cost-effective alternative to ship-borne hydrographic surveys for tropical clear-water island environments. Source: NCCR–SAC ISRO Collaborative Study — Satellite-Derived Bathymetry, Lakshadweep & Gulf of Mannar, 2024

Key Findings

The SDB framework delivered validated bathymetric depth maps for remote Indian island coastlines with quantified accuracy:

  • R² = 0.691 — Gulf of Mannar calibration
    The Log-Ratio Model achieved a coefficient of determination of 0.691 during ICESat-2 LRM calibration, demonstrating strong predictive performance for water depths in the optically shallow, clear-water environment of the Gulf of Mannar — validating the methodology as statistically reliable for tropical island bathymetry
  • Cost-effective alternative to ship surveys validated
    Satellite-derived bathymetry successfully reproduced nearshore depth contours at 10 m spatial resolution using freely available data (ICESat-2 + Sentinel-2) and open-source cloud processing — demonstrating viability as a low-cost, high-revisit monitoring solution for remote island chains where conventional hydrographic surveys are logistically prohibitive
  • Replicable framework for remote Indian islands
    The GEE-based workflow is scalable and directly transferable to other remote Indian coastal ecosystems including Andaman & Nicobar Islands, Lakshadweep atolls, and Gulf of Kutch — providing a replicable national model for satellite-based coastal depth mapping under NCCR–MoES programmes
